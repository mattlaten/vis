Introduction

Problem Description

Conventional means of searching for published papers often involve traversing through large lists of items. More often than not, one is also forced to type in many different search terms in order to find related papers. As such, users might not be fully aware of areas of research related to the one they are currently researching. This could result in users missing out on potentially important articles.

Input/Output Data

The only input data required for the system is relevant search terms relating to the field of research the user wished to find papers in. The systems yields the following output data:
  • Published papers.
  • Information related to the published papers (i.e. year published, authors, journal it was published in, etc.)
  • Areas of research related to the current field the user is considering.

Typical User Profile

Simply put, a typical user is anyone searching for published articles. In particular, users would typically be university/college students and professors. Furthermore, our system could be extended to cover any field of research, thus giving us a practically infinite user base.

Related Work

Current work relating to our system exists largely in the form of journal articles that simply display a list of relevant articles based on the search term used. Google Scholar ACM Digital Library IEEE library

Visual Queries

Our system aims at supporting the following visual queries: What fields are related to the current field of enquiry? What papers are closely related to the most relevant paper of my search? How closely related are these related papers? What kind of published papers exist in the related field of research (i.e. journal articles, books, book chapters, conference papers...)?

Design Description

Initially our system was aimed at visualising published papers at UCT (this data can be found at www.researchoffice.uct.ac.za/publication_count/stats/). In particular, our system attempted to visualise two main pieces of information:
  • Articles/reports relevancy to fields of of research
  • Number of articles published in each field
Our design was largely inspired by two existing data visualisations: Venn Diagrams: Venn Diagrams are highly effective tools at displaying both unions and intersections between different sets of data. Thus it would lend itself well to showing unions and intersections between different fields of research within UCT. Graphs: Graphs are visual representations of entities (Nodes) and connections (Edges) between pairs of said entities. Moreover, they serve as effective tools for displaying paths between any two entities. To this end it was decided that graphs would be an effective way to visualise the actual published papers and the relationships between them. Our system worked by using an “interactive drilldown”. This was essentially a process whereby the user starts at a macro level (i.e. the difference faculties of the university) and works their way down through the hierarchical structure, eventually arriving at the published papers in each field. In particular, this is: Faculty -> Department -> Fields of research within the department -> Papers published within those fields Upon reaching the level at which the fields of the department in question are visible, the relationships between the fields are displayed using Venn Diagrams. Upon selecting a field in the department, users are shown a graph representing the papers published in that particular field. In addition, relationships between the current field and other related fields are shown.

Initial Sketches

Initial Prototype

Upon finalising our initial design, a formalised low-fidelity version of our visualisation was created using Photoshop: Reaction to our initial prototype was not particularly positive. Overall, the concept of “interactive drilldown” was not well received. In terms of usability the system was not particularly efficient as it required users to sort through several pages in order to potentially find a field/paper they were interested in. Moreover, using the area of the circles to display the number of papers published in each field was erroneous. Instead, the radius of the circles should should represent the number of papers in each field. This is due to the fact that the "number of papers" published is a one dimensional measure. Area on the other hand is a two dimensional measurement. Using a two dimensional measurement to represent a one dimensional measurement results in a misrepresentation of the data.

Revised Sketches

As a result of the feedback given, the overall scope of our project was altered. Rather than simply representing published papers at UCT it was decided to create a visual representation of search results of published papers. In particular, it was decided that search results from "Google Scholar" (scholar.google.com) would be visualised. The new design still retains some similarities with the original. Venn Diagrams and Graphs still remained the largest inspiration for our visualisation. In addition, the focus of the design remained the visualisation of related articles. However there are several main differences outlined below: Rather than focusing on the UCT data set, the new design could be used to represent any desired collection of published works. The entire concept of “interaction drilldown” was completely removed. Instead users simply type in a search term and immediately view papers related to that result. Addition visualisation elements were added to the results. In particular, several distinctions were made in the way in which papers are related (to be discussed later).
